If your gums bleed every time you brush, it is easy to assume you just need a "better cleaning" and move on. For many people, understanding what LANAP is and how it treats gum disease becomes important once routine cleanings stop fixing the problem and deeper periodontal pockets start forming.

This guide explains how LANAP works, how gum disease is diagnosed, what recovery time can look like, and how to plan next steps with the Magic Smile Dental team.

## How Dentists Diagnose Periodontal Disease

Diagnosis is not based on appearance alone, because gums can look "fine" while deeper periodontal pockets continue to break down. A proper exam combines measurements, imaging, and risk-factor review to determine whether you are dealing with gingivitis or periodontitis.

Key diagnostic steps include:

- **Periodontal charting:** probing depth, bleeding on probing, attachment loss
- **Dental X-rays** to assess bone levels and rule out other causes

Periodontal charting records probing depth around each tooth, along with bleeding on probing and signs of clinical attachment loss. Dental X-rays help evaluate alveolar bone loss patterns and confirm whether bone changes match periodontal disease rather than other issues.

## What LANAP Is (and What It Isn't)

LANAP stands for **Laser-Assisted New Attachment Procedure**. It is a specific, minimally invasive dentistry approach within laser periodontal therapy that uses a dental laser alongside deep cleaning to reduce infection and support healing.

LANAP is not a "quick cosmetic laser gum treatment" meant to simply reshape tissue. The clinical goal is to reduce bacterial infection inside gum pockets and create conditions that may support new attachment, tissue regeneration, and in some cases bone regeneration, depending on the starting condition and how the body responds.

Outcomes vary because periodontal disease is not one-size-fits-all. Results depend on disease severity, pocket depths, home care, smoking status, systemic health factors like diabetes, and long-term periodontal maintenance.

### FDA Clearance and the LANAP Protocol

Patients often see the phrase "FDA cleared" when researching LANAP, and it is worth understanding what that means. In plain terms, "FDA cleared" indicates the device has met specific regulatory requirements for its intended use, which is different from a blanket promise of outcomes for every patient.

It is also important to separate the LANAP protocol from generic "laser gum treatment" marketing. The LANAP protocol refers to a standardized clinical approach using a specific laser system and defined steps, rather than a loosely described laser add-on to a cleaning.

### LANAP vs. Traditional Gum Surgery (Flap Surgery)

Traditional gum surgery often refers to flap surgery, where the gum tissue is reflected back to access deep calculus and infected areas for thorough cleaning. Flap surgery may involve sutures and can be associated with more post-op discomfort and a higher likelihood of visible gum recession, depending on the case and technique.

LANAP is generally positioned as a minimally invasive option because it aims to preserve healthy tissue while treating diseased pocket lining. That said, conventional periodontal surgery may still be recommended when anatomy, severe defects, or specific access needs make flap surgery the more predictable choice.

A dentist or periodontist may recommend traditional gum surgery when deep areas cannot be adequately treated with non-surgical methods, when tooth prognosis is poor, or when additional regenerative procedures are planned. The right approach depends on pocket depth patterns, bone levels, and how stable the teeth are.

## How LANAP Treats Gum Disease: Step-by-Step

LANAP treatment is typically organized around a clear clinical sequence, from diagnosis and treatment planning to follow-up and re-evaluation. The exact steps can vary by provider, but the core idea is consistent: reduce infection in periodontal pockets, remove calculus and biofilm from root surfaces, and support stable healing.

A typical sequence includes evaluation, anesthesia for comfort, laser decontamination, scaling and root planing with root debridement, and then healing support with follow-up care. The laser is used to target diseased tissue in the pocket lining while aiming to preserve healthy tissue.

Scaling and root planing remains a key part of infection removal because the roots must be cleaned of calculus, bacterial biofilm, and toxins. After treatment, the goal is for the tissue to heal in a way that reduces pocket depth and supports healthier attachment over time.

### Step 1: Comprehensive Exam and Treatment Planning

Before any laser periodontal therapy begins, the office should complete a full periodontal workup. This usually includes periodontal measurements, dental X-rays, and a review of your medical history to identify risk factors and medication considerations.

Comfort is typically managed with local anesthesia, and your dentist will explain what you should feel versus what you should not feel. If you are anxious, ask what additional comfort options are available and whether your health history supports them.

At Magic Smile Dental, the team commonly focuses on careful periodontal charting, clear communication, and individualized treatment planning so expectations match clinical findings.

### Step 2: Laser Decontamination and Deep Cleaning

This phase focuses on lowering bacterial load and physically removing hard deposits from the roots. One laser pass is used to address inflamed or infected pocket lining and reduce bacteria inside periodontal pockets.

After laser decontamination, the roots are cleaned through scaling and root planing, which may also be described as root debridement and root planing. The purpose is to remove calculus and biofilm so the tissue has a cleaner surface to heal against.

If you have multiple areas involved, treatment may be organized by quadrants. Your treatment plan should specify which areas are being treated and why.

### Step 3: Bite Adjustment and Healing Phase

In some cases, a bite adjustment, also called an occlusal adjustment, is recommended to reduce excessive forces on healing teeth. This matters because inflammation plus heavy bite forces can contribute to tooth mobility and slow stabilization.

During the first week, many patients notice mild soreness or sensitivity and are advised to follow a soft diet for a short time. A re-evaluation appointment is scheduled weeks later to re-check probing depth, bleeding on probing, and overall healing response.

## Benefits, Candidacy, and When LANAP May Not Be the Best Fit

People often consider LANAP because they want a less invasive approach than traditional gum surgery, and because they are concerned about gum recession. Benefits patients commonly care about include a minimally invasive approach, potentially less post-op discomfort, and shorter downtime, although recovery time is case-dependent.

LANAP may be considered for patients with moderate to severe periodontitis, deeper gum pockets, and those trying to avoid scalpel-based flap surgery when clinically appropriate. Candidacy still depends on accurate diagnosis, tooth prognosis, and commitment to follow-through.

Limitations and contraindications should be discussed honestly before scheduling treatment. Factors that can reduce predictability include uncontrolled diabetes, heavy smoking, poor oral hygiene adherence, and certain medications that affect healing or bleeding.

Implants require special consideration. LANAP is designed for natural teeth, and peri-implantitis around dental implants may require different laser protocols or other approaches rather than the LANAP protocol.

### Can LANAP Help With Severe Gum Disease?

LANAP is often used for moderate to severe periodontitis, including cases with deep periodontal pockets. Results vary based on the amount and pattern of alveolar bone loss, the starting probing depth, and whether teeth are stable or already showing significant tooth mobility.

For advanced cases, realistic goals typically include infection control, pocket reduction, stabilization, and a maintenance strategy to prevent relapse. In some situations, a tooth may still have a poor prognosis even after treatment, and your provider should explain alternatives.

### Common Mistakes That Reduce Results

Even excellent in-office treatment can fail if the aftercare and maintenance plan is ignored. The most common issues that undermine results include:

- Skipping periodontal maintenance visits after treatment
- Continuing smoking or vaping, or inconsistent brushing and flossing

Periodontal disease is chronic for many patients, meaning long-term periodontal maintenance is not optional if you want to protect the investment in treatment. Your dentist or periodontist should outline a maintenance schedule and home care tools that fit your dexterity and risk level.

## Recovery and Aftercare

Recovery after LANAP is often described as easier than traditional gum surgery, but you should still plan for a healing window. Mild soreness, gum tenderness, and tooth sensitivity are common, and temporary diet modifications can make the first few days more comfortable.

Aftercare essentials usually include gentle brushing, following any prescribed rinses if indicated, and avoiding picking at the gums. If you are given specific instructions about flossing or interdental brushes during early healing, follow those directions rather than guessing.

If anything feels abnormal, such as increasing swelling, persistent bleeding, or worsening pain, contact your dental office promptly.

### How Long LANAP Takes and Follow-Up Timeline

Many LANAP cases are completed in one or two sessions, depending on how many quadrants need treatment and how inflamed the tissue is at the start. A re-evaluation appointment often occurs in a matter of weeks to measure probing depth changes, bleeding on probing, and healing progress.

After that, periodontal maintenance is typically scheduled every 3 to 4 months initially, especially for patients with a history of periodontitis. Over time, the interval may be adjusted based on stability, home care consistency, and risk factors like smoking or diabetes.

If you are noticing bleeding gums, persistent bad breath, gum recession, or loose teeth, schedule a periodontal evaluation so you can get a clear diagnosis and a plan that matches your needs and long-term goals.
